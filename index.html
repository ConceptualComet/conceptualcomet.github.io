Pro tip for stopping doomscrolling (that actually works): Set up parental controls on your own devices, but either have someone else set the pin/password or close your eyes and mash buttons randomly.

That's it. That's all you have to do. It works on all devices, you don't need any special apps or whatever, and resetting a pin you don't know is enough of a pain that you'll only do it when you really need to.

IDK why more people don't just try this.

Watching bookbinding videos has introduced me to a huge variety of tools I never knew existed. Which got me thinking about this video essay from PhilosophyTube that explains how tools become an extension of our bodies and make us transhuman. We find we need to move beyond our limited biology, so we create an object to accomplish our goal. Sometimes that object is just a means to create another tool-object. A book, I've come to realize, is simply a tool for protecting text, and text is a tool for communication of ideas, which themselves are a sort of tool. And that ties to Hank Green's recent Vlog Brothers entry on the natural-unnaturalness of human-made technology and how we invented tools to go to the moon simply because we couldn't stop wanting to go to the moon.

Tools are pretty neat.

Curriculum

Personal curriculums have become a huge trend, and for once I think people following a trend is good! I've always loved learning, so the idea of giving it a tiny bit more structure (TINY bit) is wonderful to me.

Since everyone does their curriculum differently, I figured I should explain my approach. First, and probably most unusually, I don't follow a timeline. I follow my interest that day. Yes, this means I jump around from subject to subject a lot, but I don't view that as a bad thing. I embrace rhizomatic thinking and like drawing connections in unexpected places. I even keep all my notes in the same notebook, not organized at all, so I might have a block of writing about cult tactics on the same page as my astrophysics notes.

I also keep my curriculum pretty simple, yet academically rigerous. I like taking actual university courses (many great schools offer some free online) to give my learning an outline. But I do remind myself the goal is not to become an expert. I only want to learn as much as my interest dictates. It's supposed to be fun.

Finally, I use subjective measurements of success. Often the "output" of my learning (besides a lot of notes) is something creative, like a zine or an essay or even a built object. This very website is the output of my course on web design.

Right now (April 10, 2026), I'm really into book arts, punk culture, and secular witchcraft.

Name

Like a comet, my interests burn bright for a brief time before they fade into inky blackness, only to return once more on an orbiting path. That point when a comet reaches its shortest distance to the sun is called the perihelion.

Design

I wanted to capture the idea of ephemera collected over eons by an entity observing the Earth and delighting in human culture. Of course, space-related items would feature heavily. I purposely rejected simple design and y2k nostalgia-bait, instead embracing my own style of eclectic maximalism to make a site that feels fully me.

Technology

This is the first website I've totally built myself since high school (class of 07!), so I kept it very simple on the technology side. I handcode directly on GitHub in the browser. Yep, even the blog entries. I use Claude Code to audit for accessibility. The site is hosted on GitHub Pages. The music player is Amplitude.js. My domain name registrar is Porkbun. I don't track analytics or use cookies, etc., and there's no comment section or guestbook. This is a personal space, not a platform, so I intentionally provide only an email address.
